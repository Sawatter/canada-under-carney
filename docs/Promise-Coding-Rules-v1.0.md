# Promise Coding Rules v1.0

## What This Document Does

This document defines the rules for how promises are selected, classified, and rated in the Canada Under Carney dashboard. These rules are designed to be portable across governments — the same framework could be applied to any future PM without modification.

---

## What Counts as a Promise

A promise is included if it meets ALL of these criteria:

1. **Specific and observable** — it describes a concrete action, target, or outcome that can be verified
2. **Publicly stated** — it was made in a campaign platform, throne speech, budget, mandate letter, or official press conference
3. **Attributable to the government** — it is something the federal government committed to doing (not a hope, aspiration, or wish)
4. **Material** — it affects a meaningful number of Canadians or represents a significant policy choice

### What Gets Excluded

- Vague aspirations ("make life more affordable") without a specific mechanism
- Rhetorical commitments that are not tied to a policy, program, or legislative action
- Provincial or municipal commitments that the federal government cannot deliver unilaterally
- Promises made by individual MPs or ministers that are not government policy

### How Compound Promises Are Split

If a single announcement contains multiple distinct commitments, each is tracked separately. Example: "We will eliminate the consumer carbon tax AND preserve the industrial carbon pricing system" = two separate promises.

If a commitment is a single action with a single delivery mechanism, it is tracked as one promise regardless of how many groups it affects.

## Status Categories

| Status | Definition | Evidence Required |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Delivered** | The commitment has been fully implemented and independently confirmed. Results are measurable. | Legislation passed, program operational, target met, confirmed by independent source. |
| **In Progress** | The government has taken concrete steps (funding, legislation, program launch) but delivery is not yet complete. | Bill introduced, funding allocated, program structure announced, partial implementation documented. |
| **Stalled** | The commitment was announced but no meaningful progress has occurred, or progress has stopped. | No bill, no funding, no program activity. Or: previously active work has halted without explanation. |
| **Abandoned** | The government has formally withdrawn the commitment or taken actions that are incompatible with delivering it. | Formal cancellation, repeal of enabling legislation, or policy actions that make the promise impossible to fulfill. |
| **Thwarted** | The government attempted delivery but was blocked by external factors (opposition, courts, provinces, international actors). | Documented attempt + documented external blockage. The government must have demonstrably tried. |
| **Unclear** | The commitment is too vague to assess, the evidence is genuinely disputed, or the situation has changed enough that the original promise may no longer be relevant. | Explained in the evidence note. Used sparingly. |
| **Too Early** | Insufficient time has passed for meaningful assessment. The commitment is acknowledged but no verdict is possible yet. | Typically applies to commitments less than 6 months old or requiring multi-year implementation. |

### Status Assignment Rules

1. **Evidence, not effort.** Status reflects verifiable results, not good-faith intention. Trying hard and failing = Stalled, not In Progress.
2. **Outcome over announcement.** A press conference is not delivery. Legislation is not implementation. Implementation is not an outcome.
3. **Most conservative reasonable status.** When evidence is ambiguous, assign the lower status. It is easier to upgrade with evidence than to defend an inflated status.
4. **One status per promise.** No split ratings. If a promise is partially delivered, use "In Progress" with an evidence note explaining what remains.

## Durability Classification

Each promise is tagged with a durability level indicating how permanent the action is:

| Durability | Definition | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Legislated** | Passed into law. Survives a cabinet shuffle or election unless repealed. | One Canadian Economy Act, Building Canada Act |
| **Program** | Funded program with institutional structure. Survives the budget cycle but can be cut. | Build Canada Homes, $2B AI compute strategy |
| **Budget measure** | Tax change, rebate, or spending line in a budget. Can expire or be reversed in next budget. | Carbon tax elimination, GST rebate, grocery benefit |
| **Target** | A stated goal without a legislated mechanism. Can be abandoned without formal repeal. | NATO 2%, 500K homes/year, net-zero 2050 |
| **Framework** | A strategy, review, or planning process. Creates institutional direction but not binding outcomes. | Foreign policy review, red tape review |
| **Commitment** | A public statement of intent without a specific delivery mechanism. Lowest durability. | Buy Canadian procurement, full financial disclosure |

---

## What Evidence Changes a Status

A status moves when **new evidence meets the threshold for the new category**, not on a fixed schedule.

| From | To | Evidence Required |
|------|-----|-------------------|
| Too Early | In Progress | Concrete government action (bill, funding, program launch) |
| In Progress | Delivered | Independent confirmation of full implementation and measurable results |
| In Progress | Stalled | 3+ months without documented progress on an active file |
| Any | Abandoned | Formal cancellation or incompatible policy action |
| Any | Thwarted | Documented attempt + documented external blockage |
| Any | Unclear | Evidence becomes genuinely disputed or ambiguous |

---

## How Disputed Cases Are Handled

When a promise status is genuinely debatable:

1. The status is set to **Unclear** with a detailed evidence note explaining the dispute
2. The "Critics say / Defenders say" section on the parent dimension captures both interpretations
3. The status is revisited at the next monthly update with fresh evidence
4. If two reasonable analysts would disagree, the lower status prevails until evidence resolves the dispute

---

## Cross-Government Portability

These rules are designed to apply to any Canadian federal government. The status categories, evidence thresholds, and durability classifications do not reference any specific party, PM, or ideology. A future version of this dashboard could track a Conservative, NDP, or coalition government using the same framework without rule changes.

The test: **if these rules would produce different results depending on which party is in power — they are broken.**
