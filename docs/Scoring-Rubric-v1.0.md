# Canada Under Carney — Scoring Rubric

## Grading Methodology for Government Performance Assessment

**Version 1.0 · April 2026**

This rubric defines the criteria used to assign grades across the twelve policy dimensions evaluated in the *Canada Under Carney* report. It is designed to make grading decisions transparent, replicable, and defensible. Any reader applying these criteria to the same evidence base should reach a substantially similar grade.

---

## Grade Definitions

### A Range (3.7–4.0): Target Met or Exceeded

The government set a specific, measurable target. It was funded. Independent sources confirm delivery at or near the stated goal. The policy response matches the scale of the identified problem. Credit-claiming is supported by evidence.

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **A** | Target fully met. Delivery confirmed by independent sources. No material caveats on methodology or accounting. Credit-claiming is proportionate to actual contribution. |
| **A-** | Core target met, but with minor execution gaps, partial reliance on accounting reclassification, or delivery that meets the letter but stretches the spirit of the commitment. |

**Example from report:** Defence & Trade (A-). NATO 2% achieved and confirmed by NATO. However, the milestone was partly achieved through accounting reclassification (Coast Guard, etc.), and Canada placed in NATO's bottom third at exactly 2.0%.

---

### B Range (2.7–3.3): Clear Progress, Credible Trajectory

The policy response is substantive and directionally correct. Measurable progress is occurring, though delivery has not yet matched stated targets. The gap between commitment and result is explainable by timing, jurisdiction, or genuine external constraint — not by inaction or overclaiming. Independent assessment is broadly positive.

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **B+** | Strong progress toward target. Independent experts assess the trajectory as credible. Remaining gap is small or explained by factors outside federal control. |
| **B** | Meaningful progress with a credible plan. Delivery is behind schedule but the direction is correct, funded, and institutionally supported. |
| **B-** | Progress is real but pace is insufficient relative to the problem's scale. Trajectory is positive but will not reach stated targets without acceleration. |

**Example from report:** No dimension currently graded in the B range. Immigration (C+) is the closest — the correction is real and necessary, but the absence of a defined long-term model and the risks from the sharp pullback prevent a B-.

---

### C Range (1.7–2.3): Response Exists but Falls Materially Short

A policy framework is in place, but delivery is significantly below stated targets or the scale of the identified problem. Credit-claiming exceeds measurable results. Direction may be correct, but pace, scale, or follow-through is inadequate. Some repackaging of pre-existing initiatives may be present.

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **C+** | Genuine policy effort with partial delivery. The government is doing real work but results are significantly below what was promised or what the problem requires. Some wins are real; others are overclaimed. |
| **C** | Policy framework exists but the gap between announcements and outcomes is substantial. Repackaging of pre-existing momentum is evident. The signature policy tool has not yet been tested. |
| **C-** | Effort is itself questionable or largely performative. Response exists on paper but measurable impact is minimal. Transparency or credibility concerns weigh on the assessment. |

**Examples from report:** Major Projects (C) — institutional framework is real but credit-claiming for pre-existing projects is documented. Carbon Tax (C) — consumer elimination was a real win, but the industrial system's preservation raises scrutiny questions. Ethics & Transparency (C-) — no proven wrongdoing but the disclosure gap is itself the problem.

---

### D Range (0.7–1.3): Minimal or Inadequate Response

The response is minimal relative to the problem's scale. The gap between announcements and outcomes is large. The structural trajectory has not meaningfully changed. Policy may address symptoms rather than root causes. Measurable improvement in the lived experience of affected Canadians is negligible or absent.

| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **D+** | The government is attempting a response and the direction is partially defensible, but the scale is grossly inadequate relative to the problem, or a major policy reversal has occurred that the external context only partially justifies. |
| **D** | Delivery is a small fraction of stated targets. Structural conditions have not improved. The policy response does not match what the government itself identified as the scale of the challenge. |
| **D-** | Near-total failure on the file. Government relief covers a negligible share of the identified cost or shortfall. Structural drivers are unaddressed. The lived experience of affected Canadians has not improved. |

**Examples from report:** Fiscal Health (D) — deficit nearly doubled, no credible medium-term anchor, PBO gives 7.5% probability of meeting own targets. Housing Supply (D) — 4,000 units launched against a shortfall of hundreds of thousands. Grocery Prices (D-) — $307 benefit against a $1,600+ annual cost increase per family.

---

### F (0.0): No Response or Active Deterioration

The government either took no action on an acknowledged problem or actively made the inherited condition worse through policy choices. Reserved for cases where inaction or harm is clear and not explained by genuine external constraint.

**Note:** No dimension in the current report is graded F. The F grade exists as a methodological anchor for completeness. A government that, for example, eliminated housing programs entirely while housing starts collapsed would warrant this grade.

## Modifiers

The following contextual factors are applied consistently across all dimensions. They explain why two files with similar delivery gaps may receive different grades.

### Timing Fairness
Some policy outcomes cannot be achieved in thirteen months. Housing construction, GDP per capita, and productivity are structural challenges built over a decade or more. For these files, the grade reflects direction of travel and adequacy of effort, not completion. Files where thirteen months is sufficient to show results — defence spending, carbon tax elimination, immigration levels — are graded on outcomes.

**Decision rule:** If the government set a multi-year target, the grade assesses whether the first-year trajectory is consistent with reaching that target. If the trajectory is flat or declining, the grade reflects that, even if the timeline is short.

### Jurisdictional Limits
Housing, healthcare, and education involve provincial and municipal authority. The federal government cannot unilaterally deliver in these files. Grades reflect what the federal government can fairly control: funding levels, program design, intergovernmental partnerships, and whether federal targets are realistic given shared jurisdiction.

**Decision rule:** The federal government is graded on what it promised, funded, and executed within its authority. It is not graded down for provincial non-cooperation, but it is graded down for setting federal targets that require provincial action it has not secured.

### External Constraint
Genuine external pressures — the US trade war, global commodity prices, pandemic legacy effects — are acknowledged as context. They do not automatically raise a grade. The question is whether the response was adequate given the constraint.

**Decision rule:** External pressure justifies a slower pace but not inaction. If the government uses external constraint to explain policy reversals that go beyond what the constraint required (e.g., eliminating the tree-planting program during a trade war), the external-constraint modifier does not apply to the excess reversal.

### Credit-Claiming Penalty
If the government claims credit for outcomes it did not cause — pre-existing projects, pre-existing trends, provincial or private-sector initiatives — the grade is adjusted downward from what the raw outcome number would suggest.

**Decision rule:** The test is counterfactual. Would this outcome have occurred on a similar timeline without the government's intervention? If yes, the government does not receive full credit. The Major Projects file (C) is the clearest application of this modifier.

## Grade Movement Rules (for Dashboard Updates)

Grades change when the underlying evidence changes, not on a fixed schedule. The following rules govern when a grade moves:

### Conditions for an Upgrade (+)
- New data shows measurable improvement in the key metric(s) for that dimension
- A major policy action addresses a previously identified gap
- Independent assessment (PBO, CMHC, Fitch, OECD) upgrades its outlook
- A previously stalled commitment shows credible momentum

### Conditions for a Downgrade (-)
- New data shows deterioration in key metric(s)
- A commitment is formally abandoned or scaled back
- Independent assessment downgrades its outlook
- A gap between announcement and delivery widens

### Conditions for No Change (=)
- New data is consistent with the existing trend
- No major policy action has occurred
- The dimension is in a "too early to judge" holding pattern

### Documentation Requirement
Every grade change must be accompanied by: (1) the specific new evidence that triggered the change, (2) which rubric criterion applies, and (3) the date of the change. This creates an auditable history.

## Aggregate Grade Calculation

The overall grade is calculated as the unweighted GPA average of all twelve dimensions on a 4.0 scale.

An alternative pocketbook-weighted grade is also published. This double-weights the four dimensions that most directly affect household economic life: Fiscal Health, Housing Supply, Grocery Prices, and GDP & Productivity.

Both grades are reported. The unweighted grade is the headline. The pocketbook-weighted grade is disclosed as context. The choice of weighting is an editorial judgment, and readers who believe household economics should carry more weight will reasonably interpret the pocketbook-weighted grade as more representative.

---

## How to Use This Rubric

This rubric is published alongside the report and dashboard so that:

1. **Readers** can assess whether they agree with the grades by applying the same criteria
2. **Critics** can challenge a specific grade by pointing to which criterion they believe was misapplied
3. **Future updates** are constrained by the same framework, preventing grade drift based on shifting editorial mood
4. **Other analysts** could apply this rubric to a different government or a different time period and produce comparable results

The rubric is versioned. If criteria change, the version number increments and the change is documented.
